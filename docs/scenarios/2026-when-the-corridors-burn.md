# 1. DRAMATIZED OPENING

The corridors did not start burning all at once. They were made to burn.

By March 2026, Gaza is still the moral center of the world and still the place most of the world is trained not to see. The dead are counted in hospitals that no longer have enough electricity to preserve dignity, in family notebooks written beside camp stoves, in the testimony of medics, in the lists that survive because someone refuses to let a name vanish. The Israeli war-state under Benjamin Netanyahu has not treated Gaza, the West Bank, and Lebanon as separate theatres. Israel Katz talks corridor control and expulsion politics as if civilian life were a logistics variable. Likud, Religious Zionism, and Otzma Yehudit drive an atmosphere in which the IDF is not simply an army but the armed expression of a whole political order that wants Palestine broken into units of manageability: raidable district, sealed crossing, emptied neighborhood, controllable camp, ungrievable dead.

The United States does not arrive to stop this. Donald Trump, Pete Hegseth, John Ratcliffe, Karoline Leavitt, U.S. Central Command, OFAC, and the wider sanctions-and-legal architecture do what the Atlantic system has always done when its corridor order is threatened: they declare that escalation is stability, that bombardment is deterrence, that blockades are administrative, that the world must reorganize itself around the movement of carriers, insurers, bomb inventories, underwriters, and official lies. The White House calls direct war with Iran strength. The Treasury treats Venezuela and Cuba not as societies under punishment but as files to be tightened. The DOJ anti-Cuba and anti-Venezuela apparatus turns extraterritorial pressure into procedure. None of this is reducible to a single speech or a single president. It is a system with spokesmen, ministries, command structures, courts, brokers, data platforms, and clearinghouses.

That is why this is a corridor war. It is not only missile against missile, or state against state. It is Gaza under siege, south Lebanon under bombardment, the West Bank under raids, the Red Sea under maritime fear, Hormuz under insurance panic, Suez under delay, Havana under financial siege, Caracas under oil coercion, and the Black Sea under another war that keeps feeding this one. It is Maersk and MSC route calculations. It is Trafigura and Glencore pricing risk. It is BlackRock and Vanguard treating devastation as portfolio turbulence. It is Palantir turning surveillance into target acceleration. It is Meta, X, and Google sorting which testimony moves and which one dies in moderation queues or search burial. It is Rupert Murdoch and Vincent Bollore tightening narrative discipline across media ecosystems that call exterminatory policy self-defense and call anti-war refusal extremism.

From Gaza, this looks different than it does from Washington, London, or Tel Aviv. It looks like a mother at Rafah measuring medicine against one more night. It looks like medics in Khan Younis and the north trying to move witness before the connection dies. It looks like neighborhood committees in the West Bank mapping raids, prison sweeps, and settler violence so that the attack can still be named as structure, not chaos. It looks like people in south Lebanon realizing that the northern front is being widened so that Palestinian life can be treated as one disposable component in a larger operational theater. It looks like Ansar Allah turning the Red Sea into a reminder that shipping routes are political, not natural. It looks like Hezbollah and the IRGC being folded into an escalation vocabulary that is supposed to hide the prior fact of Israeli and U.S. violence.

The fracture states around the fire do not become heroes. Egypt under Abdel Fattah el-Sisi remains a regime-survival state first, a pressured mediator second, and a Rafah gatekeeper always. Egyptian General Intelligence can pass names, move negotiations, and open a temporary slit in the wall, but it can also help reseal the wall. Jordan under King Abdullah II is a buffer monarchy with Palestine on its doorstep and regime continuity in its every calculation. Its hospitals, border logic, and security apparatus can either preserve witness or trap it in containment. Qatar under Tamim bin Hamad Al Thani remains a mediation channel whose usefulness depends on whether the wider war allows negotiation to breathe. Turkey under Recep Tayyip Erdogan and Hakan Fidan condemns, maneuvers, hedges, bargains, and measures every move against NATO contradiction and regional rivalry at once. None of these states sit cleanly in an anti-war camp. All of them are pressured by Palestine. All of them are shaped by corridor politics. None are centered above the people who are being bombed, displaced, sanctioned, and blockaded.

The Gulf monarchies also do not become a single bloc in this story. Saudi Arabia, the UAE, Bahrain, and Kuwait form a host-and-target subsystem: basing, finance, insurance, oil movement, sovereign survival, and retaliation risk tied together. They host architecture that makes war possible and then discover that architecture can make them targets. A strike in the Gulf is not just a military event. It is an insurer event, a port event, a labor event, a refinery event, a fear event. It shakes the belief that distance can be bought by alignment. Bahrain is especially exposed because host logic is dense there. Kuwait can hedge. The UAE can try to reroute and absorb. Saudi Arabia can posture between host and distance. None escape blowback entirely.

Europe also fractures without innocence. Pedro Sanchez and Jose Manuel Albares give public expression to a visible break in the Atlantic consensus, and Spain matters here not because Madrid will save anyone but because open refusal weakens the lie that NATO Europe speaks with one escalatory voice. The UK under Keir Starmer moves the other way, closer to the Atlantic establishment instinct that every crisis must be metabolized into alliance discipline, military readiness, and the normalization of a larger war horizon. Russia’s war on Ukraine continues, and each attack in the Black Sea theatre keeps grain, ports, energy, and militarism inside the same system of pressure. China watches while U.S. allies worry about overextension, not because they are suddenly anti-imperial, but because too many open fires can destabilize the corridor order they rely on.

And in the western hemisphere the same logic widens. Nicolás Maduro, the PSUV, Venezuelan communes, Cuban Communist Party structures, CDRs, and neighborhood defense networks are not separate from Palestine in this scenario. They are linked by method. Sanctions, banking restrictions, oil waivers, legal coercion, media discipline, and official threat are different instruments of the same imperial management of circulation and refusal. Cuba and Venezuela are not annexes to the main story. They are among the weak that the system punishes whenever it needs to prove that sovereignty without obedience will be made materially expensive.

So the protagonists here are not governments. They are Palestinian sumud committees, medics, witness networks, prisoner-letter chains, port workers, communal kitchens, neighborhood defense structures, anti-war organizers, grain-solidarity channels, and corridor labor that refuses to let war move smoothly. They are the people who know that a convoy is political, that a clinic is political, that a shipping delay is political, that testimony is political, that neighborhood survival under blockade is political. They know the corridors because they live where the system seals them shut.

When the corridors burn, victory is not clean. Gaza is not suddenly free. Lebanon is not suddenly safe. The blockade against Cuba does not disappear. Venezuela is not relieved of coercion because one campaign succeeds. But the system can be made to lose smoothness. A gate can be forced open. A route can be delayed. A witness archive can survive deletion. A monarchy can be pushed from host to hedge. A public fracture can widen inside Europe. A sanctioned people can keep breathing long enough to remain politically present. That is what this scenario asks players to hold: not fantasy rescue, but the costly, collective work of keeping life, memory, and sovereignty moving while the world’s corridor managers try to close every passage.

# 2. SCENARIO THESIS

**Thesis:** *2026 — WHEN THE CORRIDORS BURN* models a plausible world-war condition in which the same systemic bloc moves through bombardment, sanctions, logistics, finance, media management, and maritime disruption. The scenario is grounded in documented March 2026 escalation and then projects a 4-8 week widening in which Gaza, Lebanon, Hormuz, Suez, Cuba, Venezuela, and the Black Sea become one connected field of pressure.

**Why this is a WTSCO scenario:** the protagonists are not states or generals. They are movements, medics, communes, witness structures, dockworkers, neighborhood defense formations, and anti-war networks. Secret Mandates matter because survival, witness, sovereignty, and corridor strategy do not perfectly align. Victory remains partial and costly; defeat remains political rather than nihilistic.

**What makes this scenario distinct from generic WWIII fiction:** it refuses the fantasy that history is driven only by great-power chess. This is not "Trump vs Iran," nor is it a neutral clash-of-civilizations frame. It is a corridor war in which Palestine remains the moral center, Venezuela and Cuba are integral to the same imperial logic, and regional powers are treated as contradictory state formations rather than clean camps.

# 14. LAUNCH CAMPAIGN MODEL

- Roll `2d6`.
- Base target: `8+`.
- Add modifiers from:
  - committed Comrades: `+1` per `2` committed
  - committed Evidence: `+1` per Evidence
  - Global Gaze: `+1` per full `5`
  - War Machine: `-1` per full `4`
  - home region bonus
  - faction domain bonus
  - support card bonus (`+2`)
  - active system pressure modifiers
- Success bands:
  - `11+ total`: surge, remove `2 Extraction`, gain `+1` domain
  - `8-10 total`: success, remove `1 Extraction`, gain `+1` domain
  - `6-7 total`: attention failure, no extraction removed, `Global Gaze +1`
  - `<=5 total`: backlash, no extraction removed, `War Machine +1`
- `WarMachine` domain campaigns can lower the global `War Machine` by `1` on strong success.
- Public victory cannot resolve before `round 4`, even if conditions are met earlier.

# 20. NARRATIVE ARC

Early game feels claustrophobic. The coalition is not trying to win history. It is trying to stop the map from closing. Gaza looks one card away from breach. The Caribbean looks like a slow strangulation. The Gulf and Red Sea threaten to absorb every action with new panic.

Midgame reveals the true shape of the scenario: pressure that looked local is actually systemic and pressure that looked abstract can be turned. Egypt at four matters to Gaza. Saudi posture matters to Hormuz. A Spanish fracture matters to War Machine campaigns. A dock strike can matter to Palestine. A Cuban memory brigade can matter to coalition score.

Endgame moral pressure is about what counts as breakthrough under catastrophe. Liberation does not mean peace. Symbolic victory does not mean safety. It means enough corridors opened, enough witness preserved, enough sovereignty defended, enough people kept politically present that the system does not get to close the file.

Liberation feels like survival made historical. Symbolic victory feels like a chain of impossible signals held together just long enough to prove the weak are still here. Defeat feels like the map tightening again: more sealed crossings, more exhausted organizers, more blockaded breath, but not the end of the struggle.

# 22. BALANCE RISKS AND MITIGATIONS

| Risk | Likely player experience | Mitigation |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Palestine front becomes impossible | Coalition feels punished for centering Gaza | Keep Gaza highest pressure but pair it with the strongest relief valves and two dedicated factions |
| Caribbean front feels tacked on | Cuba / Venezuela read as side theater | Keep full factions and a full front, with multiple dedicated cards and mandates |
| Regional powers overwhelm player agency | Players feel like they are waiting on states | Make tracks player-responsive and threshold-based; states alter passage, not win conditions |
| Billionaire naming feels superficial | Name-dropping without structural clarity | Tie each billionaire to a firm, media ownership pattern, or platform function |
| Scenario becomes too state-centric | Movements disappear behind diplomacy | Put every win path through labor, witness, communes, or neighborhood structures |
| Crisis overload causes early collapse | Losses before the game becomes legible | Release tuning lowered seeded extraction and several system extractions; keep deterministic tests on startup pressure |
| Symbolic path too easy or too hard | Either trivial beacon rush or impossible all-beacon lock | Keep three active Beacons but tune each threshold in relation to live fronts and domains |
| Fast cadence undercuts emotional arc | Game wins before the full corridor-war feel lands | Post-release cadence pass can raise turn length without re-hardening the scenario to unwinnable levels |

# 23. FINAL RECOMMENDATION

The strongest recommended version is the **release-tuned hard mode now in the repo**: keep the five-faction slate, the six-front map, the regional-powers subsystem, and the real-actor card architecture exactly as implemented, while treating the current pacing as acceptable for v1 and a candidate for later cadence tuning.

Implement first: scenario content, registry wiring, world-map board definition, and deterministic startup tests. Simulate first: mixed-mode autoplay on `when_the_corridors_burn` at small batches, then longer runs after every pressure change. Test first in playtesting: whether Gaza still feels central once the Caribbean and shipping fronts are active, whether Secret Mandates create productive tension, and whether the regional subsystem reads as nuanced rather than state-heroic.

Required advisory review: Palestinian movement-centered review, Cuba / Venezuela anti-sanctions framing review, and a political-sensitivity pass on named billionaires, media owners, and regional states to keep the scenario sharp without caricature.
